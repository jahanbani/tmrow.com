A pragmatic guide to Climate Change

If you see anything inconsistent, or would like to propose a change, feel free to edit the page on github or submit an issue.

This guide is a short and pragmatic guide to what Climate Change is, what the drivers behind it are, and what we, as individuals, can do about it.

Global temperatures are rapidly increasing

"Warming of the climate system is unequivocal, and since the 1950s, many of the observed changes are unprecedented over decades to millennia", writes the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) in its fifth assessment.

Temperature anomalies
°C compared to 1951-1980 average
Source: NASA's Goddard Institute for Space Studies (GISS) (data)

Sixteen of the 17 warmest years have all occurred since 2001 (with the exception of 1998). Furthermore, NOAA reported in August 2016 that each of the last 16 months were the warmest they've ever measured. The measured warming has been found to be consistent across seasons and regions.

We have now achieved a temperature increased of 1°C since the end of the 70s. A change of 1°C in 50 years is unprecedented, as it can be seen from the following reconstruction of temperatures in the last 11 000 years:

Temperature anomalies in the last 11 300 years
°C compared to 1951-1980 average
Source: A Reconstruction of Regional and Global Temperature for the Past 11,300 Years (data) before 1880, NASA's Goddard Institute for Space Studies (GISS) (data) after 1880

What is worrying is not so much the change itself, but rather the speed at which this change is taking place. Historically, a change of 1°C seems to happen in thousands of years - not decades. The Earth is a complex ecosystem, and disturbing it by as little as a couple of degrees in the blink of decades (or even centuries) is a heavy disturbance.

Climate change seems serious, but what causes it?

Climate change is caused by greenhouse gases

Some gases are transparent to the sun's incoming rays heating the Earth, but are opaque (acting as mirrors) with respect to outgoing rays emitted back from the Earth. Those gases act as a blanket, trapping heat that is unable to escape, and are called greenhouse gases due to their warming behaviour. This greenhouse effect stabilises our climate, keeping nights relatively warm despite behind deprived of sunlight.

One of those greenhouse gases is carbon dioxide (CO2). By drilling ice cores (columns of ice containing small bubbles of old trapped air), we have been able to infer historical CO2 concentration up to 800 000 years BC. This concentration has been relatively stable until the industrial revolution, where it has been skyrocketing. Again, what is worrying is the speed at which the change of concentration is happening. Both the concentration level and the speed of change are unprecendented in the history of measurements:

By comparing the heating and cooling components between the pre-industrial period (where the greenhouse gas atmospheric levels had not yet increased) and now, we observe that the drastic increase in greenhouse gases is the main driver behind the observed warming, although some other effects cause a certain amount of cooling:

Relative changes in radiative forcing in 2011 compared to 1750
in W/m2
Source: Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) WG1 AR5, Fig. 8.15 p697 (data)

It is important to state that some greenhouse gases will keep warming the Earth many years after they have been emitted, because they do not disappear instantaneously. Up to 40% of the CO2 emitted today will still be present 1 000 years from now, thus still causing warming (see FAQ 12.3 here). If we were to stop emissions today, CO2 levels would essentially never come back to its pre-industrial level on time scales relevant for our society.

This is in stark contrast with the immediate and ephemeral cooling effect of volcanos eruptions or aerosols that stay in the atmosphere for a very short time. It is therefore very important to reduce greenhouse gas emissions very quickly, as they will affect the climate in the centuries (even millennials) to come. So how do we do it?

Greenhouse gases are the result of human industrialisation

Historically, the world's energy has come from burning trees (biomass) for heat and tool manufacturing. However, the invention of an efficient steam engine by James Watt in 1784 meant humans were now able to convert much more effectively fossil fuels (coal, and later oil and gas) into mechanical work, such as lifting heavy objects or turning the wheels of a very heavy train.

In particular, the discovery of oil, being both light and dense, gave access to a powerful and portable source of energy. The energy released when burning 1 liter of oil (roughly 10 kWh) is equivalent to 25 professional athletes cycling for one hour (at 400 W each). 1 liter of oil weighs under 1 kg and costs 100 to 1000 times less than the equivalent human labor. Thus intensive human labour was replaced by machines, propelling humanity into an industrialised age where the use of fossil fuels displaced jobs from agriculture, to the service sector.

World primary energy consumption since 1800
in EJ (exajoules)
Source: Our World In Data (data)

Note that there has been very little substitution between the different energy fuels. The discovery of a new fuel (be it coal, oil or gas) didn't replace the fuel used previously, but has instead fueled additional growth. Also note that even though renewable energy is a decent part of newly installed energy production facilities nowadays (we hear about it very often in the news), it is still only a tiny percent of global energy supply. In fact, we see that in 2015, 83% of our energy still comes from fossil fuels, which surprinsingly is exactly the same as in 1980: the growth of renewables has been matched by (or even outpaced by) a similar growth of fossil fuels.

Every day, an average person on Earth uses roughly 60 kWh of energy per day. That's the equivalent of dedicating, to each person, 15 professional cyclists for 10 hours a day. The current quality of life that we are enjoying has been enabled by the efficient use of fossil fuels freeing us intensive labour.

Unfortunately, burning fossil fuels is a process that releases carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere, explaining the drastic increase in measured CO2 concentrations. That it happening as soon as we drive a car, take a plane or use electricity. It also indirectly happens when we consume objects assembled from parts transported from afar, or when we use plastics, chemicals, lubricants, and pharmaceuticals (all derived from oil). Objective: 2 tons CO2eq per human per year by 2050

All greenhouse gases differ by how much radiation they trap and by how long they stay in the atmosphere. To be able to compare them, we convert the mass of any greenhouse gases into the mass of CO2 that would yield an equivalent warming over 100 years. That unit is called CO2 equivalent, abbreviated CO2eq. For example, 1g of N2O (nitrous oxide, used in fertilisers) corresponds to 300gCO2eq.

The Paris agreement [ref needed] has set the world the 2°C objective at horizon 2100. This gives us a carbon budget [ref], which is x% of proven reserves. [dangerous to let the free market...]. This also means we don't have time to wait for fusion to be around, or another new cheap abundant energy source.

So how do we personally do to reach that objective? Do we recycle more? Eat organic? Vote for more wind turbines? In this jungle of complex decisions, some pragmatism has to be introduced, in the form of a sober quantification of the impact of the different choices we make.

It all starts with figuring out how much you are emitting compared to the 2t objective. Here's an estimate of personal emissions depending on where you live:

In the next sections, we're going to give an overview of the impact of some daily decisions we make related to transportation, food and electricity. We will present comparable quantities in order to show which behaviour changes can have the most impact.

Finally it is important to understand that no activity is carbon-free. Even the production of renewable energy requires the construction of wind turbines and solar panels, which will emit greenhouse gases during transportation, assembly, and mining of the necessary rare metals.

In order to get rid of fossil fuels, we must electrify transportation directly by using electric motors, or indirectly, by using hydrogen produced by electricity. Electricity: remove coal, gas and oil power plants

Greenhouse gas intensity of electricity production modes
in gCO2eq / kWh consumed
Source: Live cycle emissions from Electricity Map (data) and How Sustainable is Stored Sunlight? for solar + storage intensity estimations.

It is crucial that we get rid of fossil fuels for electricity generation. To keep track of how we stand, check out the Electricity Map. Low-carbon strategies fall into two categories in regions where the terrain does not allow the installation of large-scale hydropower: nuclear and wind/solar. Nuclear has a waste problem and wind/solar suffer from intermittency, and must therefore incorporate a storage solution in order to deliver electricity continuously.

If you're living in a country with high renewable penetration, you can reduce the need for storage by consuming electricity when the wind blows, or when the sun shines. Even better: have your heating systems and electric car do it automatically.

It is important to note that although doing differently might help, reducing your absolute level of consumption might be the only way to get to the 2t objective. Even a low-carbon activity can cumulate to great amounts when repeatedly carried out.

Information must be accessible and widely spread

How to we get a whole civilisation to shift consumption patterns? We need to put in place incentives that reward low-carbon consumption decisions motivating people to stay under the 2t yearly budget. Money is the strongest incentive we have, and thus, a carbon tax must be set into place. It could very well replace the VAT (Value-Added Tax), such that the total amount of tax is kept equal but simply skewed such that low-carbon products are less taxed.

So far, the carbon tax has only been applied to large companies and manufacturers (with an insufficient price). A carbon tax on goods consumed (like a VAT) would be complicated to set in place, because it would require all companies of the value chain to implement precise carbon accounting.

We believe that the consumer should be made aware, and rewarded for positive action. We believe that by giving consumers an idea of the impact of their consumption habits, we empower them to make better choices and have an impact.

What is needed is to centralise our global knowledge about greehouse gas intensities of various goods or services consumed, and build interfaces that enable any actor to access it and present it to consumers.

It is our belief that when consumers become both aware of the objective and empowered enough to achieve it, we will see a shift in consciousness.
