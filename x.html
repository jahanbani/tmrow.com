A pragmatic guide to Climate Change

A very short introduction

Global temperatures are rapidly increasing

"Warming of the climate system is unequivocal, and since the 1950s, many of the observed changes are unprecedented over decades to millennia", writes the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) in its fifth assessment.

Source: NASA's Goddard Institute for Space Studies (GISS) (data)

It is very clear that the global average temperature is increasing as sixteen of the 17 warmest years have all occurred since 2001 (with the exception of 1998). Furthermore, NOAA reported in August 2016 that each of the last 16 months were the warmest they've ever measured. The measured warming has been found to be consistent across seasons and regions.

We have now reached a warming of 1°C since the end of the 70s. A change of 1°C in 50 years is unprecedented, as it can be seen from the follow reconstruction of temperatures in the last 10 000 years:

Source: A Reconstruction of Regional and Global Temperature for the Past 11,300 Years (data)

What is worrying is not so much the change itself, but rather the speed at which this change is taking place. Historically, a change of 1°C seems to happen in thousands of years - not decades. The Earth is a complex ecosystem, and disturbing it by as little as a couple of degrees in the blink of a decades (or even centuries) is a non negligible disturbance.

Climate change is caused by greenhouse gases

Some gases are transparent to the sun's rays heating the Earth, but are opaque (acting as mirrors) with respect to rays emitted back from the Earth. Those gases act as a blanket, trapping heat that is unable to escape, and are called greenhouse gases due to warming behaviour. The most abundant greenhouse gas is water wapor and is part of the natural stability of our climate. By trapping heat inside the atmosphere, it enables our nights to be relatively warm compared to the cold of outer space.

One of the most interesting greehouse gas is carbon dioxide (CO2), as it has been relatively stable until the industrial revolution. We know this because we have been able to drill Ice Cores that are columns of ice containing small bubbles of old trapped air. By analysing this air, we can infer the CO2 concentration in previous times. This major increase during the industrial revolution has been found to be the main driver behind temperature increases. We know this because climate models fail to reconstruct temperature increases without taking into account the recent increase in greenhouse gas concentration.

Source: National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) Ice Core records (data) and Mauna Loa records (data)

Greenhouse gases are the result of human industrialisation

The greenhouse effect has been identified since the beginning of the century. This article from 1912 is one of the earliest accounts, which further identifies the risk of releasing additional greenhouse gases, like carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere, which could cause an increase in temperatures. The invention of an efficient steam engine by James Watt in 1784 meant humans were able to effectively convert fossil fuels into energy, a process that releases carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere. Fossil fuels have a tremendous amount of energy stored. In terms of X, 1L of oil represents Y, Z. The discovery of the use of fossil fuels propelled humanity into an industrialised age, and enabled us to have the living standard we have now. Currently, each human on earth has the equivalent of X energetic slaves working to them to produce what we consume, ultimately sustaining our living standard.

Several greenhouse gases exist, differing by how much radiation they trap per unit area and unit time, and by how long they stay in the atmosphere. To be able to compare them, the so-called global warming potential of each gas is calculated, being the amount of heat trapped by a certain mass of gas compared to a similar mass of CO2 over 100 years.

Source: Global warming potential (Wikipedia) from 2013 IPCC AR5 p714
